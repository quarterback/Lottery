That's not realistic for the chip window specifically, because the chip window changes behavior.
A few things that should introduce the right kind of randomness:
Roster volatility within the window. Some bad teams sign motivated vets or call up G-League guys who play above their heads for 15 games. Model this as a random "hot streak" modifier — maybe 20% of bad teams get a +8 to +15% win probability bump for a random 5-8 game stretch. This represents the real phenomenon: a team that's 20-55 but suddenly competitive because they're actually trying.
Strategic bidding variance. Right now teams near the bottom auto-bid 25 because they have nothing to lose. But in reality some franchises are conservative by front office culture, some are erratic, some misread matchups. Add a bidding personality randomly assigned to each team — conservative, aggressive, or volatile — that affects both bet size and timing, independent of chip level.
Opponent effects. A playoff team mailing in a back-to-back against a desperate lottery team is a genuine upset scenario. Model a fatigue/motivation discount for playoff teams in chip games — maybe 30% of their chip window games they're -10% win probability because they're resting players ahead of the postseason.
The "nothing to lose" rally. Teams at or near the chip floor with 10+ games left should have a distinct behavior mode — they've essentially hit rock bottom and can swing aggressively every game without further downside. That should produce a meaningful comeback rate, maybe 15-20% of floor teams rally into the top half of the leaderboard.
